About

Hi, I'm Asha. I am a multidisciplinary technologist specializing in software development, human factors, and project management. I was a software engineer for 10 years, as an individual contributor and manager, before deciding to take a sabbatical for academic pursuits. I've always been interested in people, the what and why humans do what they do, and I wanted to learn how to use that knowledge to build better products and systems. During my sabbbatical, I obtained a second bachelors in Psychology and I'm currently a masters student at Embry-Riddle (graduating with a M.S. of Human Factors at the end of 2025). This allows me to integrate both my passions for technology and psychology.

I have a special interest in civic tech, healthcare, and human-centered design.

My software engineering experience encompasses fullstack development, architecture design, project management, and leadership. My primary focus was backend development, working on tools for fulfillment logistics, payments infrastructure, internal tools, and API development. I’ve also built internal dashboards and reusable components in React. My favorite projects involve talking to users, understanding how they use a tool, learning about issues they encounter, and finding ways to solve their problem. I also have experience in leading teams as a project manager and engineering manager.

My human factors research has concentrated on issues in health, medical devices, human-computer interaction, and wearable technology. The integration of emerging technologies in healthcare creates interesting opportunities for how we can leverage these systems to optimize performance, while exposing the challenges to building this technology within the boundaries of human cognition.

Outside of professional pursuits, my hobbies are running, cycling, ceramics, and cooking.

The Effect of News Exposure on Impression Formation

research

An experiment on the effect of reading positive and negative news on subsequent impression formation. Participants were randomly assigned to read a negative or neutral news article, then completed a questionnaire with photos of strangers asking them to rate how strongly they agreed or disagreed with positive statements about the stranger.
